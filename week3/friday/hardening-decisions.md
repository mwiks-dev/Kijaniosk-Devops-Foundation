# KijaniKiosk Production Hardening Decisions

The production foundation for KijaniKiosk was designed to make the server safe from the first build, not safe only after someone notices a problem. That distinction matters because this system supports a payments workflow, which means security, stability, and operational clarity are business concerns, not just technical ones. The design choices below aim to reduce the chance that a configuration mistake, a weak service boundary, or a routine maintenance action turns into an outage or a security incident.

The first decision was to separate the platform into dedicated service identities. The API, payments, and logging functions do not run as a single shared identity and do not inherit broad system authority. This matters because a weakness in one service should not automatically become a weakness in the whole server. If one component fails or is misused, the others should remain insulated as much as possible. The risk this reduces is the spread of impact from one compromised or misconfigured process to the rest of the platform.

The second decision was to tightly control access to configuration and secret material. Settings that define service behavior and credentials that support service-to-service communication are necessary for operation, but they are also sensitive. Broad access to them would increase the chance of accidental disclosure or unauthorized use. Restricting access keeps the system easier to trust and easier to explain to reviewers. The risk this reduces is the exposure of production credentials and configuration to people or processes that do not need them.

The third decision was to define shared access deliberately instead of relying on loose default permissions. Shared operational data, especially logs and health artifacts, often become insecure because teams choose convenience first and control later. In this design, shared access is intentional. Each service receives only the level of access it needs, and operational visibility for support work is preserved without turning shared data into a public resource on the server. The risk this reduces is the silent accumulation of over-permissioned data and the operational confusion that comes from inconsistent access rules.

The fourth decision was to harden how services interact with the host system. Each service is constrained so that it can perform its intended function without having broad influence over the wider machine. This is especially important for the payments component because it handles the most sensitive business workflow. Restricting filesystem access, reducing visibility into unrelated host resources, and removing unnecessary privileges all shrink the damage a compromised service could cause. The risk this reduces is privilege escalation and misuse of host-level resources by an application process.

The fifth decision was to reset the network policy to reflect present intent rather than past troubleshooting history. Over time, manually edited network rules often become a record of previous incidents instead of a clear statement of what traffic should be allowed. The final policy makes that intent explicit: public web access where needed, administration where required, internal health visibility only for the expected source, and denial of direct access where proxying is the intended pattern. The risk this reduces is accidental service exposure and hard-to-diagnose network behavior caused by stale or conflicting rules.

The sixth decision was to treat logs and journals as controlled operational assets rather than unlimited byproducts. Logs are vital for troubleshooting and auditability, but unlimited growth can itself create service instability. The design therefore keeps useful history while placing limits on storage growth and automating rotation. This also preserves evidence during incidents without letting logging become the cause of a new outage. The risk this reduces is disk pressure, loss of useful history, and repeated failures caused by unmanaged operational data growth.

The seventh decision was to build verification into the provisioning process itself. A script completing is not, by itself, proof that the resulting system is ready. The final process checks service identity state, permissions, firewall rules, logging behavior, and health output before declaring success. This matters because confidence should come from evidence, not assumption. The risk this reduces is false-positive deployment success, where the platform appears complete but fails on first real use.

The eighth decision was to narrow human administrative access. Operational staff still need enough authority to support the system, but unrestricted privilege makes mistakes harder to contain and reviews harder to trust. The selected approach gives operators the access needed for support tasks without turning every operational action into full control of the machine. The risk this reduces is human error, privilege misuse, and weak accountability for administrative actions.

Taken together, these decisions create a platform that is easier to defend, easier to reason about, and easier to rebuild consistently. That consistency matters both for engineering and for governance. A stronger posture is not just one with more restrictions; it is one where each restriction clearly maps to a real business risk and where the final system can be verified rather than merely described.

| Control | What it does | Risk mitigated |
|---|---|---|
| Separate service identities | Runs API, payments, and logging under different identities | Limits blast radius if one service is compromised |
| Restricted config access | Narrows who can read service configuration and secrets | Reduces credential and configuration exposure |
| ACL-based shared access | Grants precise access to shared operational data | Prevents overexposure while preserving necessary operations |
| `ProtectSystem=strict` | Reduces what service processes can change on the host | Lowers host modification risk from application processes |
| `NoNewPrivileges=true` | Prevents services from gaining more privilege after start | Reduces escalation opportunities |
| Intent-based firewall rules | Allows only the traffic the design actually requires | Prevents accidental exposure and stale-rule drift |
| Journal persistence and rotation | Preserves evidence while controlling storage growth | Reduces disk exhaustion and loss of incident history |
| Verification phase | Confirms the final state instead of assuming it | Prevents false-ready deployments |
| Restricted operator privileges | Limits administration to approved support tasks | Reduces human error and excessive privilege |
| Health artifact generation | Produces a structured record of provisioning health | Improves operational visibility and audit confidence |

This posture still has limits. It does not replace secure application code, dependency patching, external monitoring of provider infrastructure, or broader incident response maturity. It also does not eliminate all denial-of-service scenarios or every form of insider misuse. What it does provide is a controlled and reviewable foundation: the server starts from a constrained state, exposes less by default, and produces stronger evidence when it is claimed to be ready.
